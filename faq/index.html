Know before you go

Nepal, answered

The practical stuff every traveller asks: visas, insurance, signal, altitude, money. Rates are indicative as of 2026; confirm the current details with official sources before you fly.

Arrival & visas

Do I need a visa, and can I get one on arrival?

Most nationalities get a tourist visa on arrival at Kathmandu's Tribhuvan International Airport and major land borders. Bring a passport valid for at least six months, a passport photo, and USD cash: about $30 for 15 days, $50 for 30 days, or $125 for 90 days. You can also apply online beforehand or at a Nepali embassy; extensions are handled at the immigration offices in Kathmandu and Pokhara.

Do I really need travel insurance?

Yes, and it has to be the right kind. Nepal's trekking-permit system expects insurance that explicitly covers high altitude (up to roughly 6,000 m) and emergency helicopter evacuation; ordinary policies often cap around 2,500 m and won't qualify. Helicopter rescue is the standard response to serious altitude illness, so this coverage is genuinely important, not a box-tick.

Money & tipping

Can I use cards, or where are the ATMs?

Cards and ATMs are reliable in Kathmandu and Pokhara but rare to non-existent on the trails. Carry enough Nepali rupees in cash for the whole trek (teahouse meals, snacks, device charging and tips are cash-only up high) and keep small notes for tipping. Withdraw and break larger bills before you leave the city.

How much should I tip guides and porters?

Tipping is customary and makes a real difference. A common guideline is roughly $15–20 per day for a lead guide and $10–15 per day for each porter, handed over as a lump sum at the end of the trek (in rupees, in an envelope) rather than daily. It's appreciated rather than obligatory, so tip what feels right for the service you got.

Staying connected

Will I have phone signal and internet on the trek?

Buy a local SIM at the airport or in town for a few dollars: Ncell and Nepal Telecom (NTC) both sell tourist data packs (around रू500–1,000 for 5–10 GB). Ncell tends to be stronger in Kathmandu and the lower valleys; NTC is often more reliable at altitude. Coverage reaches surprisingly far on popular routes (Everest to Namche, Annapurna to Manang) but expect dead zones higher up, and teahouse Wi-Fi is usually paid and slow.

What about power and plugs for charging?

Nepal runs on 230 V with type C, D and M sockets, so bring the right adapter. On treks, teahouses charge a small fee to charge devices and power can be patchy, so a power bank (ideally two) is the single most useful thing you'll pack for keeping a phone alive for photos, maps and offline navigation.

Health & safety

Is altitude sickness a real risk?

Yes. Acute mountain sickness (AMS) can affect anyone above roughly 2,500–3,000 m, regardless of fitness. The defence is pacing: ascend slowly, build in acclimatisation days, sleep low, stay hydrated, and never ignore headache, nausea or breathlessness, because descending is the only reliable cure. Our planner builds an acclimatisation curve into every route for exactly this reason.

Can I drink the water?

Don't drink the tap water. Use a filter, purification tablets or a UV purifier, or refill at the boiled-water and treatment stations many teahouses now run, cheaper and greener than buying plastic bottles all the way up. Sealed bottles and hot drinks are safe fallbacks.

Do I need a guide, or can I trek solo?

Since 2023, solo trekking is restricted in Nepal's national parks: most popular routes now require a licensed guide, and restricted areas like Manaslu and Upper Mustang need a registered agency plus special permits. Beyond the rules, a good guide handles permits, navigation, altitude decisions and emergencies, which is why every trek here comes with a verified one.

Timing

When is the best time to trek in Nepal?

The prime windows are autumn (October–November) and spring (March–April): stable weather, clear mountain views and comfortable temperatures. Winter is cold and quiet but fine on lower routes; the June–September monsoon brings rain, leeches and cloud, though rain-shadow regions like Mustang and Dolpo stay trekkable.
